## Where Reviewers Agree

Every expert in 2026 agrees on one thing: **smart appliances are now mainstream, but the value proposition is mixed.** WiFi connectivity is becoming standard even at mid-range prices, making it harder to avoid smart features even if you don't want them.

Experts also agree that some smart features are genuinely useful while others are gimmicks. The useful ones: remote oven preheating, cycle completion notifications on washers and dryers, and diagnostic alerts. The gimmicks: refrigerator cameras, recipe suggestions, and most touchscreen interactions that are easier on your phone.

## Where Reviewers Disagree

**Smart refrigerators — innovation or problem?** This is the most polarizing category. Samsung's Family Hub and LG's InstaView with touchscreens are flagship products that generate excitement. But Yale's repair data consistently shows smart refrigerators have higher service rates. Consumer Reports rates them highly on features while acknowledging the complexity. Repair technicians are generally skeptical.

**Planned obsolescence:** Some experts warn that smart appliances face a software support problem — manufacturers may discontinue app support for older models, turning your smart appliance into a regular one with extra electronics that can fail. Others argue that major brands (Samsung, LG, GE) have committed to long-term software support.

**Voice assistant integration:** Alexa and Google Assistant integration is growing across all appliance categories. Some experts view it as the future of kitchen interaction. Others point out that shouting at your oven from across the house is a solution looking for a problem.

## The S&D Smart Appliance Paradox

Here's the counterintuitive truth about smart appliances and S&D: **smart appliances depreciate the fastest, which means S&D savings are the largest.**

A $3,500 Samsung Family Hub refrigerator might be $1,500-2,000 at a S&D retailer — 40-55% off. Compare that to a basic model that might be 30% off. The more features and tech an appliance has, the steeper the S&D discount tends to be.

This creates an interesting opportunity for tech-savvy buyers: if you want smart features and can verify the electronics work, S&D is the smartest way to buy them.

**But the risk is real:**
- Touchscreen damage is expensive to repair ($400-800 for panel replacement)
- WiFi module issues may not be visible during a quick store inspection
- Smart features add 15-25% to repair rates based on Yale's data

## Smart Feature Tier List

Based on expert consensus, here's what's worth having and what isn't:

**Worth it:**
- Cycle completion notifications (washers, dryers, dishwashers)
- Remote oven preheat and temperature monitoring
- Diagnostic codes sent to your phone for service calls
- Energy usage monitoring

**Marginal value:**
- Internal cameras (refrigerators)
- Recipe integration and cooking guides
- Voice assistant control

**Not worth the risk:**
- Large touchscreen displays (highest failure component)
- Smart dispensing/AutoDose features
- Complex multi-zone cooking automation

## S&D Buying Checklist for Smart Appliances

If you're buying a smart appliance at S&D, follow this expanded inspection:

1. **Connect to WiFi** at the store and verify the app pairs successfully
2. **Test every smart feature** — don't just check the mechanical functions
3. **Verify the model is still supported** by the manufacturer's app
4. **Check the touchscreen** for dead spots, unresponsive areas, or display artifacts
5. **Ask about the return policy** — smart appliance issues may not appear immediately
