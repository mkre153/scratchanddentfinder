Ranges and ovens are one of the smartest categories to buy scratch and dent. Here's why: once a range slides into place between your countertops, the sides and back are completely hidden. That big dent on the left panel that knocked 40% off the price? Nobody will ever see it. Not you, not your guests, not your in-laws.

But ranges also involve heat, gas lines, and electrical connections, so you need to know what to check before you hand over your money. This guide walks you through exactly what damage to accept, what to walk away from, and how to inspect a scratch and dent range like a pro.

### Why Ranges Are a Great Scratch and Dent Buy

The math is straightforward. Ranges retail anywhere from $800 to $3,000 or more. At a 30-50% discount, you're saving $250 to $1,200 — and in most cases, the damage that earned that discount is invisible after installation.

The most common type of damage on scratch and dent ranges is dents on the side panels. These panels get dinged during shipping and warehouse handling. Since ranges sit between cabinets or against a wall, those side panels are never visible in your kitchen. It's savings for damage you literally cannot see.

Slide-in ranges hide even more damage than freestanding models because they sit flush with cabinetry on three sides. If you're buying a slide-in, you can accept damage on the sides and back with total confidence.
</Callout>

### Gas vs. Electric: What to Inspect

The inspection process differs slightly depending on whether you're looking at a gas or electric model.

**Gas Ranges:**
Gas ranges require a few extra checkpoints, but don't let that intimidate you. The gas connection and internal valves are well-protected inside the unit. Cosmetic dents on the exterior almost never affect gas components. Here's what to verify:

- Turn on every burner and confirm it ignites cleanly
- Check the gas inlet area on the back for any visible damage or dents
- Look at the oven igniter — open the oven, start it, and confirm it lights within 60-90 seconds
- Inspect knobs and controls to make sure they turn smoothly and click into position

**Electric and Induction Ranges:**
Electric ranges have their own checkpoints:

- Examine the glass cooktop carefully for cracks, chips, or scratches — even hairline cracks can worsen with heat
- Test each heating element or burner zone to confirm it cycles on
- Check that the oven heats to the set temperature (bring a cheap oven thermometer if you can)
- Verify digital displays and touch controls respond properly

<Callout type="warning">
A cracked glass cooktop is a deal-breaker. Even a small crack can spread under heat, and replacement glass panels often cost $300-$500 — wiping out your scratch and dent savings entirely.
</Callout>

### The Oven Door Seal Test

This is the single most important check for any range, gas or electric. The oven door seal (also called a gasket) keeps heat inside the oven. A compromised seal means uneven cooking, longer preheat times, and wasted energy.

Here's how to test it: close the oven door and look for any visible gaps between the seal and the frame. Run your hand along the edges — you shouldn't feel warm air escaping when the oven is heating. If the door doesn't close flush or the seal looks warped, compressed, or torn, walk away.

### Don't Forget the Hidden Costs

Before you calculate your savings, factor in the extras. Delivery for a range typically runs $50-$100 since they're heavy. Electric ranges may need a power cord (usually $20-$40 — many ranges don't include one). Gas ranges need a gas connector kit ($15-$25) and professional hookup ($100-$200) if your current line doesn't match.

Haul-away of your old range adds another $25-$75 at most dealers. Ask about all of these costs upfront so your budget stays accurate.
