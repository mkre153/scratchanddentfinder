Everyone knows scratch and dent appliances are cheaper. But how much cheaper? Are we talking a modest 10% discount, or can you actually save enough to matter?

Let us get specific. No vague promises — real numbers by appliance type.

### The Overall Picture

Scratch and dent savings generally fall between 20% and 70% off the retail price. Most deals land in the 30-50% range. That is not a rounding error on a $2,000 refrigerator — that is $600 to $1,000 back in your pocket for a brand-new appliance with a dent you will hide against the wall.

The exact discount depends on three things: the type of appliance, the severity of the cosmetic damage, and where you buy it. Let us break each one down.

### Savings by Appliance Type

Here is what you can realistically expect to save across the major appliance categories:

**Refrigerators** save the most in raw dollars because they have the highest retail prices. A mid-range French door refrigerator retailing at $2,400 commonly shows up in scratch and dent for $1,200-$1,700.

### How Damage Grade Affects the Price

Not all scratch and dent is priced the same. Retailers grade cosmetic damage, and the grade directly drives the discount:

- **Grade A (barely noticeable):** 20-30% off. You might need to hunt for the imperfection. These sell fast.
- **Grade B (minor but visible):** 30-45% off. A scratch on the door or a small dent on the side. Most buyers find this the sweet spot.
- **Grade C (clearly visible):** 45-60% off. Noticeable dents or multiple scratches. Fully functional, just not pretty.
- **Grade D (significant cosmetic damage):** 50-70% off. Large dents, deep scratches, or damaged trim pieces. Still works perfectly but looks rough.

<Callout type="savings">
A Grade C or D appliance that gets tucked into a built-in cabinet or pushed against a wall gives you maximum savings with zero visible compromise.
</Callout>

### In-Store vs. Online Pricing

Here is something many shoppers overlook: scratch and dent appliances are almost always cheaper in person than online.

Why? Because shipping a dented appliance is a liability for the retailer. They risk additional damage in transit, returns, and unhappy customers who expected the dent to be smaller than it looked in photos. Stores would rather give you a deeper discount to walk it out (or arrange local delivery) than deal with the logistics of shipping it across the country.

<Callout type="tip">
Visit your local scratch and dent stores in person. You will often find 10-15% deeper discounts than what the same retailers list online.
</Callout>

### What Drives the Discount Higher

Several factors can push your savings even deeper:

- **End-of-model-year clearance:** When new models arrive (typically September-November), outgoing scratch and dent inventory gets aggressive discounts.
- **Holiday weekends:** Memorial Day, Labor Day, and Black Friday often add promotional discounts on top of the existing scratch and dent markdown.
- **Negotiation:** Many stores, especially independents, have room to negotiate. If you are buying multiple units, ask for a package deal.
- **Older inventory:** Units that have been sitting for months get marked down further. Ask the sales associate what has been on the floor the longest.

### Watch Out for Inflated MSRP

One caution: some retailers calculate the "percentage off" from an inflated manufacturer's suggested retail price that nobody actually pays. A refrigerator with a $3,000 MSRP that typically sells for $2,200 might be listed as "50% off" at $1,500 — which is really about 32% off the actual street price.

<Callout type="warning">
Always compare the scratch and dent price against the current sale price at competing retailers, not just the MSRP on the tag.
</Callout>

### The Bottom Line

Scratch and dent savings are real and substantial. On a single appliance, you are looking at $200 to $1,200 in savings depending on the category and damage grade. Outfit an entire kitchen and you could save over $2,000.

The key is knowing what drives the discount — damage grade, store type, timing, and negotiation — and comparing against real street prices rather than inflated MSRP.
